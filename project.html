Loss and Damage - The Unequal Cost of Climate Change

Aims

Inspired by COP-27, I am looking quantitatively at the story behind the Loss and Damage Fund requested by developing countries. My aim is to show, through data, the impact of climate change. I want to bring attention to countries who are currently causing and have caused the most climate damage. I will investigate current evidence and vulnerability of climate change, and quantify the costs that are brought by climate disasters.

CO2 Emissions

Figure 1 - CO2 Emissions per Capita

Figure 1 shows oil producing countries like Qatar have the highest CO2 emissions per capita, almost 7x the amount of the UK and 1300x the amount of the Democratic Republic of the Congo. At first glance these oil producing countries look like they should shoulder the responsibility for climate change.

Figure 2 - Cumulative CO2 Emissions

However Figure 2 shows that since 1750 The United States is by far the largest emitter, with China a distant second. Looking again at Qatar, their cumulative emissions equal just 2.7% of the United Kingdom's cumulative emissions.

This shows the imbalance of cumulative emissions where countries like Bangladesh, who are particularly vulnerable to flooding, barely move off the X-axis. Hence, we have to compare the largest emitters to whole continents and geographical areas. Here I am bringing attention to the countries that are most responsible for CO2 emissions.

The data for Figure 1 was downloaded as a CSV file from Our World in Data. Figure 1 was analysed in Excel and the data for Figure 2 was analysed using Python. I restricted the dataset just to the Areas I needed.

Extreme Weather

Figure 3 - Extreme Weather Events

Extreme weather events are defined as weather at the extremes of the historical distributions; weather that is unexpected, unusual, severe, or unseasonal. Figure 3 shows a clear general upwards trend in the number of extreme weather events. This correlates with the steep increase in global carbon emissions since 1960, Figure 2.

Figure 4 - Extreme Weather Events Broken Down Into Disaster Sub-Type

Figure 4 shows the frequency of different extreme weather events. All 6 natural disasters have large economic costs associated with them and can be extremely destructive. E.g. the 2022 flooding in Pakistan which caused damage exceeding USD 14.9 billion. Evidence of correlation suggests we should expect an increase in frequency of extreme weather events, and an increase in corresponding economic losses as a result.

The data for Figures 3 & 4 was downloaded as a CSV file from EM-DAT, The International Disaster Database. Python was used to analyse the data, creating and tallying Disaster Sub-Types in long form.

Vulnerability to Climate Change: Will These Costs Be Evenly Distributed?

Vulnerability measures a country's exposure, sensitivity, and ability to adapt to the negative impact of climate change. It measures the overall vulnerability by considering it in six life-supporting sectors – food, water, health, ecosystem service, human habitat, and infrastructure.

Overall Risk Index summarizes a country's vulnerability to climate change and other global challenges in combination with its readiness to improve resilience, giving an overarching sense of the impacts of climate change. Readiness here measures a country’s ability to leverage investments and convert them to adaptation actions.

Conclusion

We can see from Figures 5, 6 & 7 that there are differing costs and vulnerabilities to climate change. Costs, Figure 6, have a wide-ranging split between Adaption and Residual for different areas. Central Africa and parts of Asia are the most vulnerable to climate change despite low carbon contributions.

Despite having contributed the most to climate change, the US and Europe are not particularly at risk to the damages caused.

The economic cost of climate change should not be underestimated, inaction could cost the world’s economy USD 178 trillion by 2070. As shown, the effects of climate change are not just unequal but also inequitable. Loss and Damage funds should be a necessary part of future environmental discussions paid by developed countries to developing countries.
