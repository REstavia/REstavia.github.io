Loss & Damage - The Unequal Cost of Climate Change

Aims

Inspired by the recent COP-27, I chose to look quantitatively at the story behind the Loss & Damage Fund requested by developing countries. My aim was to show through data the impacts of climate change, bringing attention to countries who are currently causing and have caused the most damage to the climate. I studied data from the natural world exploring current evidence of climate change, then vulnerability to climate change, and finally quantifying the costs that are brought by climate disasters through Loss & Damage. I wanted to show that developing countries will be forced to foot the majority of the bill for problems they played a minor part in causing.

Extreme Weather

Figure 3 - Extreme Weather Events

Extreme weather events are defined as weather at the extremes of the historical distributions; weather that is unexpected, unusual, severe, or unseasonal. Figure 3 shows a clear general upwards trend in the number of extreme weather events. This correlates with the steep increase in global carbon emissions since 1960, Figure 2.

Figure 4 - Extreme Weather Events Broken Down Into Disaster Sub-Type

Figure 4 shows the frequency of different extreme weather events. All 6 natural disasters have large economic costs associated with them and can be extremely destructive. 2021 floods in Pakistan caused damage exceeding USD 14.9 billion. Evidence of correlation suggests we should expect an increase in frequency of extreme weather events, and an increase in corresponding economic losses as a result.

The data for Figures 3 & 4 was downloaded as a CSV file from EM-DAT, The International Disaster Database. Python was used to analyse the data, creating and tallying Disaster Sub-Types in long form.

Vulnerability to Climate Change: Will These Costs Be Evenly Distributed?

Vulnerability measures a country's exposure, sensitivity, and ability to adapt to the negative impact of climate change. It measures the overall vulnerability by considering it in six life-supporting sectors – food, water, health, ecosystem service, human habitat, and infrastructure.

Overall Risk Index summarizes a country's vulnerability to climate change and other global challenges in combination with its readiness to improve resilience, giving an overarching sense of the impacts of Climate change. Readiness here measures a country’s ability to leverage investments and convert them to adaptation actions.

Conclusion

We can see from Figures 5, 6 & 7 that there is differing costs and vulnerabilities to climate change. Costs, Figure 6, have a wide-ranging split between Adaption and Residual for different areas. Central Africa and parts of Asia are the most vulnerable to climate change despite low carbon contributions.

Despite having contributed the most to climate change, The US and Europe are not particularly at risk to the damages caused.

The economic costs of climate change should not be underestimated, inaction on climate change could cost the world’s economy USD 178 trillion by 2070. As shown, the impact of climate change is therefore not just unequal but inequitable. Loss and Damage funds should be a necessary part of future environmental discussions paid by developed countries to developing Countries.
